Introduction

Throughout the last few decades, women's hockey has transitioned from being a predominantly Canadian sport to one being played throughout North America, Europe, and parts of Asia. With the development of international tournaments, Olympic participation, and organizations such as the IIHF, it has gained recognition and popularity worldwide.

The Start of International Competition

In 1987, the first Women's World Hockey Tournament was held. While this was an unofficial tournamenet, it was the start of an era, one that paved the way for official tournaments starting just three years later. It was held in Toronto, Ontario and hosted teams from six countries and one comprised entirely of players from Ontario, with Canada winning gold. (La Rose, 2017)

The first officially sanctioned women's world championship, the 1990 IIHF Women's World Championship was born out of the success of its predecessor, hosted in Ottawa, Ontario. Canada wins its second gold medal in international women's hockey, and its first in an official tournament. (HHOF, n.d.)

Women's Hockey in the Olympics

In 1988, women's hockey becomes an offical Olympic sport, and team USA defeats Canada to win the first ever Olympic gold medal for the sport. (HHOF, n.d.)

It's just Canada and USA, Until it Isn't

Until 2006, Canada and USA were the only two teams who had ever played in a gold medal game, until Sweden upset team USA in the semifinals round of the 2006 Olympic Winter Games, going on to claim the silver medal.

To date, this is the only time a team other than Canada or USA had been in the gold medal game, and was not repeated until 2019 when Finland upset Canada in the semifinals of the IIHF Women's World Championship in a dramatic and controversial shootout loss, after a potential game-winning goal for Finland was overturned in overtime for goaltender interference. (Murphy, 2019)
